* From People as Recipients to Partners in Health Creation: A Perspective on Bridging the Evidence-to-Health Gap:
** Abstract
Despite unprecedented advances in biomedical science, clinical research and evidence-based healthcare, improvements in population health have frequently fallen short of expectations. This persistent disconnect between the generation of evidence and the realization of better health outcomes may be understood as an **Evidence-to-Health Gap**—the gap between what is known to improve health and what people are ultimately able and willing to implement in their daily lives. While considerable effort has focused on strengthening evidence generation, dissemination and implementation, comparatively less attention has been given to the central role of people as the primary agents through whom health is ultimately created.

We argue that the persistent Evidence-to-Health Gap may be partly explained by insufficient partnership with people throughout the evidence-to-action continuum. Rather than viewing individuals primarily as recipients of healthcare, participants in isolated clinical decisions or targets of behavioural interventions, we propose a broader partnership model in which people participate in defining problems, identifying priorities, generating evidence, translating knowledge into accessible forms, implementing evidence within the realities of everyday life and continuously improving health-creating actions through feedback and shared learning. This Perspective proposes that since health is ultimately created in people's daily lives and that people are the primary agents of health creation, scientific research, healthcare professionals and health systems should aim to to support, enable and strengthen people's capacity to create, preserve and restore health.

This perspective integrates concepts from evidence-based medicine, lifestyle medicine, implementation science, person-centred care, public health, citizen science, shared decision making and learning health systems into a unified conceptual framework centred on partnership. The proposed framework extends beyond disease prevention and healthcare delivery towards improving LifeQuality by recognising that rights and responsibilities are inseparable and that sustainable health creation depends upon both empowerment and accountability. We conclude by outlining implications for research, policy and practice, and propose a research agenda for evaluating partnership as a foundational principle for translating scientific evidence into measurable improvements in individual and population health.

** Introduction)

Much of modern healthcare remains organised around episodes of illness, clinical encounters and institutional delivery of services. Although these functions are indispensable, they represent only a fraction of the circumstances in which health is created or lost. The biological consequences of nutrition, physical activity, sleep, stress management, substance use, social relationships, environmental exposures and adherence to evidence-informed recommendations accrue largely outside healthcare settings through decisions and behaviours enacted repeatedly in everyday life. Consequently, the ultimate effectiveness of scientific discovery depends not only upon the quality of evidence generated but also upon people's capacity, motivation and opportunity to translate that evidence into sustained action. This observation has become increasingly evident across chronic diseases, where behavioural and lifestyle factors contribute substantially to both disease burden and health improvement.^12–15^

Recognition of the importance of implementation has stimulated major advances in implementation science, dissemination research and knowledge translation.^16–18^ These disciplines have expanded understanding of how evidence can be incorporated into healthcare systems, professional practice and policy. Similarly, person-centred care and shared decision making have strengthened recognition of patients' values, preferences and autonomy within clinical encounters.^19–22^ Yet these approaches generally remain anchored within healthcare delivery rather than encompassing the broader continuum through which health is created in everyday living.

The concept of health creation has received growing attention within public health and salutogenic models, emphasising assets, capabilities and the social conditions that enable individuals and communities to flourish rather than focusing exclusively on disease prevention.^23–25^ Lifestyle Medicine similarly demonstrates that many leading causes of morbidity and premature mortality are strongly influenced by modifiable behaviours supported by robust scientific evidence.^26–28^ Nevertheless, translating this evidence into durable behavioural change remains one of the greatest challenges in contemporary healthcare. Knowledge alone rarely produces sustained action; individuals must navigate competing priorities, social influences, economic constraints, environmental barriers and personal motivations that shape health-related decisions over the life course.^29–31^

These observations suggest that the Evidence-to-Health Gap cannot be understood solely as a problem of evidence production or implementation. It may also reflect an incomplete conceptualisation of the role of people within the health ecosystem. People are often engaged after evidence has been generated, when recommendations are communicated and implementation is expected. By contrast, comparatively less emphasis has been placed on partnering with people throughout the entire evidence-to-action continuum—from identifying the questions that matter most, through evidence generation and synthesis, to contextual adaptation, implementation, evaluation and continuous improvement. Existing movements, including patient and public involvement in research, citizen science and community-engaged participatory research, have demonstrated that meaningful partnership can improve the relevance, acceptability and uptake of research while strengthening trust between science and society.^32–36^ However, these initiatives remain fragmented across disciplines and have not yet been integrated into a comprehensive conceptual framework for health creation.

We therefore propose that the persistent **Evidence-to-Health Gap** may be partly explained by insufficient partnership with people throughout the evidence-to-action continuum. This proposition does not diminish the essential roles of scientific research, healthcare professionals or health systems. Rather, it repositions these institutions according to their fundamental purpose: to support, enable and strengthen people's capacity to create, preserve and restore health. Health is ultimately created in people's daily lives. People are the primary agents of health creation.

Within this perspective, partnership extends beyond participation or consultation. People participate not only in applying evidence but also in defining the problems, asking the questions, generating the evidence, translating the knowledge and continuously improving the actions through which healthier lives are created. Evidence should be synthesised into forms that are openly accessible, understandable and relatable to people's situations and needs. Such synthesis requires not merely simplification but contextualisation, ensuring that scientific knowledge remains rigorous while becoming meaningful and actionable within diverse personal, cultural and socioeconomic circumstances.

This perspective also recognises that partnership combines respect with accountability: empowering people while acknowledging their shared responsibility for the choices through which health and **LifeQuality** are progressively created. Rights and responsibilities are inseparable. Respect for autonomy does not diminish personal responsibility, nor does responsibility justify inequity or abandonment. Instead, effective partnership requires reciprocal commitment among individuals, healthcare professionals, researchers, communities and institutions, each contributing according to their capacities and responsibilities.

Importantly, the proposed framework extends beyond disease prevention and healthcare utilisation towards the broader objective of improving **LifeQuality**. Health is valued not merely as the absence of disease but as a dynamic resource that enables individuals to pursue meaningful lives, participate fully in society and realise their aspirations. Scientific evidence therefore achieves its greatest value when it contributes to healthier, more fulfilling lives rather than remaining confined to publications, guidelines or healthcare systems.

The remainder of this Perspective develops this partnership hypothesis, presents a conceptual model for bridging the Evidence-to-Health Gap, situates the proposal within existing scholarly traditions, and discusses implications for research, policy and practice. Rather than replacing established paradigms, the framework seeks to integrate and extend them around a unifying principle: that sustainable improvements in health and LifeQuality depend upon recognising people not primarily as recipients of healthcare but as partners in health creation.

# Introduction

The past century has witnessed extraordinary advances in biomedical discovery, clinical medicine and public health. Vaccination, sanitation, antibiotics, advances in surgery, imaging, genomics and evidence-based therapeutics have transformed healthcare and substantially improved life expectancy in many parts of the world. Simultaneously, rigorous methods for generating and evaluating scientific evidence have become firmly embedded within healthcare through evidence-based medicine, systematic reviews and clinical practice guidelines. These developments have been complemented by implementation science, quality improvement, patient engagement and learning health systems, all seeking to improve the translation of scientific knowledge into better health outcomes.¹–⁹

Yet an important paradox remains. The expanding volume of scientific knowledge has not translated proportionately into healthier lives. Non-communicable diseases continue to account for the majority of premature mortality worldwide, while unhealthy diets, physical inactivity, tobacco use, harmful alcohol consumption, inadequate sleep, chronic stress and adverse social determinants continue to drive much of the global burden of disease. Although effective preventive and therapeutic interventions for many of these determinants are already well established, their sustained adoption remains limited across populations.¹⁰–¹⁴

This disconnect extends beyond prevention. Across many conditions, evidence-based recommendations often fail to achieve their anticipated impact because they are inconsistently implemented, incompletely adopted or insufficiently sustained in the settings where people actually live their lives. Consequently, a substantial gap persists between what scientific evidence demonstrates to be beneficial and the health that people ultimately experience.

This Perspective refers to this persistent disconnect as the **Evidence-to-Health Gap**: the gap between available scientific evidence and the creation, preservation and restoration of health in people's everyday lives.

Numerous disciplines have addressed important components of this challenge. Evidence-based medicine has focused on generating trustworthy evidence and integrating it with clinical expertise and patient values. Implementation science has investigated methods for improving adoption of evidence-based interventions. Public health has addressed behavioural, environmental and social determinants of health. Shared decision making has strengthened patient participation in clinical care, while learning health systems have sought to accelerate continuous improvement through systematic learning from practice. Collectively, these fields have contributed substantially to improving healthcare and health outcomes.¹–⁹,¹⁵–¹⁸

However, these approaches largely concentrate on particular stages of the evidence-to-action continuum. Comparatively less attention has been directed towards the final and most consequential stage: the continuous process through which people transform evidence into healthier lives through countless decisions and actions undertaken in homes, schools, workplaces and communities.

Health is ultimately created in people's daily lives.

People are the primary agents of health creation.

Scientific research, healthcare professionals and health systems exist to support, enable and strengthen people's capacity to create, preserve and restore health.

These propositions do not diminish the indispensable contributions of biomedical research, clinical care or public health. Rather, they reposition these institutions within a broader purpose: enabling people to create, preserve and restore health throughout everyday living. Clinical care remains essential whenever prevention is insufficient or disease occurs. Nevertheless, for much of the health experienced over a lifetime, decisive actions occur outside healthcare facilities through daily behaviours, relationships, environments and community contexts.

From this perspective, people should not be regarded merely as recipients of healthcare or passive beneficiaries of scientific evidence. Instead, they should be recognised as active partners whose participation is fundamental throughout the continuum by which evidence is transformed into health.

# Explaining the Evidence-to-Health Gap

The concept of an evidence-to-practice gap has long been recognised within medicine and implementation science. Research has consistently demonstrated that effective interventions are frequently underused, delayed in adoption or inconsistently implemented.¹⁹–²² Numerous frameworks have therefore focused on improving dissemination, implementation fidelity, organisational readiness and healthcare quality.

While these efforts have yielded substantial advances, they predominantly examine how evidence moves into healthcare systems and professional practice. The ultimate purpose of scientific evidence, however, is not merely successful implementation within organisations but improved health experienced by people themselves.

The **Evidence-to-Health Gap** therefore represents a broader construct. It encompasses every stage through which scientific knowledge is transformed into healthier lives—from identifying relevant health problems, generating evidence and synthesising knowledge to communicating evidence, supporting informed decisions, enabling sustained behaviour change and continuously learning from lived experience.

This broader perspective acknowledges that translation does not end when evidence reaches clinicians, healthcare organisations or public health programmes. Translation reaches completion only when evidence contributes to the creation, preservation or restoration of health in people's daily lives.

Numerous interacting factors contribute to this gap. Scientific evidence may not address the questions most relevant to people's everyday challenges. Evidence is frequently communicated in technical language that limits accessibility outside professional communities. Health information often remains fragmented across multiple sources that vary widely in quality and trustworthiness. Even when evidence is available, structural barriers—including education, health literacy, socioeconomic disadvantage, environmental constraints and competing life priorities—may limit people's capacity to act upon it. Sustaining healthier behaviours also requires ongoing motivation, supportive environments and adaptive learning rather than one-time exposure to information.²³–²⁸

These observations suggest that the Evidence-to-Health Gap cannot be explained solely by shortcomings in evidence generation or implementation within healthcare systems. Rather, it reflects the cumulative effects of multiple discontinuities across the entire evidence-to-action continuum, particularly where scientific knowledge intersects with the realities of everyday life.

** Explaining the Evidence-to-Health Gap (continued)

Although numerous determinants contribute to the Evidence-to-Health Gap, they share a common feature: each represents a weakening of the connection between scientific knowledge and people's capacity to translate that knowledge into healthier lives. Consequently, the challenge is not solely one of producing better evidence or implementing it more efficiently within healthcare systems. It is equally a challenge of enabling people to understand, evaluate, adapt and sustain evidence-informed actions within the realities of everyday living.

This Perspective therefore proposes that the Evidence-to-Health Gap should be viewed as a systems challenge extending across the entire evidence-to-action continuum. Scientific discovery, evidence synthesis, guideline development, healthcare delivery, public health programmes, digital technologies, education, community organisations and people themselves all contribute to this continuum. Weakness at any stage diminishes the likelihood that evidence will ultimately improve health.

Importantly, recognising the central role of people does not imply transferring responsibility for health exclusively to individuals. Health behaviours are shaped by biological, psychological, social, economic, commercial and environmental influences, many of which lie beyond individual control. Governments, healthcare organisations, educational institutions, employers and communities therefore retain essential responsibilities for creating conditions that enable healthier choices. The present Perspective argues not for individualisation of responsibility but for a more balanced partnership in which institutional responsibilities and personal agency are mutually reinforcing.

Within this broader framework, the success of scientific evidence should ultimately be judged not only by publication, citation, guideline inclusion or implementation within healthcare systems, but by its contribution to healthier lives and improved LifeQuality. This shift in emphasis encourages greater alignment between research priorities, healthcare delivery, public health action and the lived realities of the people they are intended to serve.

## Central Partnership Hypothesis

This Perspective advances the following central hypothesis:

**The persistent Evidence-to-Health Gap may be partly explained by insufficient partnership with people throughout the evidence-to-action continuum.**

Specifically, we propose that sustainable improvements in health can be accelerated when people are recognised not merely as recipients of healthcare or adopters of evidence, but as active partners throughout the processes by which knowledge is created, translated, implemented and continuously improved.

People participate not only in applying evidence but also in defining the problems, asking the questions, generating the evidence, translating the knowledge and continuously improving the actions through which healthier lives are created.

This hypothesis extends rather than replaces existing paradigms. Evidence-based medicine established the importance of integrating the best available evidence with clinical expertise and individual values. Implementation science has advanced methods for improving adoption of evidence-based interventions. Shared decision making has strengthened collaboration between clinicians and patients. Learning health systems have demonstrated the value of continuous organisational learning. Lifestyle medicine has highlighted the importance of sustained health behaviours, while public health has long recognised the influence of social and environmental determinants. Together, these disciplines provide essential foundations upon which a broader partnership model can be built.

The distinctive contribution of this Perspective is to integrate these complementary developments within a single organising principle centred on health creation. Health is ultimately created in people's daily lives. People are the primary agents of health creation. Scientific research, healthcare professionals and health systems exist to support, enable and strengthen people's capacity to create, preserve and restore health.

Accordingly, partnership begins before evidence is generated and continues long after it is implemented. Research priorities become more relevant when informed by people's lived experiences. Evidence becomes more applicable when generated with meaningful public participation. Translation becomes more effective when evidence is communicated in forms that are usable beyond professional audiences. Implementation becomes more sustainable when people are equipped to adapt evidence within their own lives and communities. Continuous improvement becomes possible when lived experience informs subsequent cycles of research, policy and practice.

Evidence should be synthesised into forms that are openly accessible, understandable and relatable to people's situations and needs.

Scientific evidence has little potential to improve health if it remains inaccessible, incomprehensible or disconnected from the contexts in which people make everyday decisions. Advances in evidence synthesis, plain-language communication, digital technologies and open science provide opportunities to transform complex scientific knowledge into practical resources that support informed action while preserving scientific integrity.

Partnership also requires reciprocity. Partnership combines respect with accountability: empowering people while acknowledging their shared responsibility for the choices through which health and LifeQuality are progressively created.

Respect acknowledges individual autonomy, lived experience and the legitimate role of people in decisions affecting their health. Accountability recognises that opportunities for improved health are realised through actions taken by individuals, families, communities, professionals and institutions working together. Rights and responsibilities are inseparable.

The objective therefore extends beyond disease prevention towards improved LifeQuality. Health creation encompasses preventing disease, preserving health, restoring function after illness and continuously improving physical, mental and social wellbeing throughout the life course. By positioning people as partners rather than passive recipients, this Perspective offers a unifying conceptual foundation for integrating research, healthcare, public health and community action in pursuit of sustained improvements in health and LifeQuality.

** 2. Explaining the Evidence-to-Health Gap

The remarkable expansion of biomedical knowledge over the past century has transformed the diagnosis, treatment and prevention of disease. Advances in epidemiology, clinical trials, systematic reviews and evidence-based guidelines have substantially strengthened the scientific foundation of healthcare. Yet the translation of this growing body of evidence into sustained improvements in individual and population health remains incomplete. Across diverse settings, substantial differences persist between what scientific evidence demonstrates can improve health and what is ultimately achieved in everyday life. We refer to this disconnect as the **Evidence-to-Health Gap**.

The Evidence-to-Health Gap extends beyond the widely recognised evidence-to-practice gap. Whereas the latter primarily concerns whether healthcare professionals adopt evidence-based interventions within clinical practice, the Evidence-to-Health Gap encompasses the entire pathway through which scientific knowledge is transformed into healthier lives. It asks a broader question: *How does reliable evidence become better health for people?*

This distinction is important because health is ultimately created not within research laboratories, policy documents or healthcare facilities, but in the daily lives of individuals and communities. Scientific evidence may identify effective interventions, healthcare professionals may recommend them, and health systems may facilitate their delivery, yet health benefits are realised only when evidence is translated into actions that are adopted, sustained and integrated into everyday living. The endpoint of translation is therefore not the publication of evidence, the development of guidelines or even the delivery of healthcare, but measurable improvements in health and **LifeQuality**.

Multiple factors contribute to this gap. At the level of research, studies may not always address questions that are most relevant to people's lived experiences or priorities. Evidence may be generated under controlled conditions that differ substantially from the complexity of everyday life. At the level of knowledge translation, scientific findings are frequently communicated in technical language that is inaccessible to many people, while recommendations may not adequately reflect differences in culture, literacy, socioeconomic circumstances or personal goals. Within healthcare systems, organisational constraints, limited consultation time and fragmented care may further impede effective implementation. Beyond healthcare, environmental, economic and social determinants often influence whether evidence-informed choices are practical or sustainable. Together, these factors help explain why the existence of high-quality evidence does not automatically result in improved health outcomes.^1–8^

Importantly, these barriers do not imply shortcomings in scientific research or healthcare delivery. On the contrary, enormous progress has been made in strengthening research quality, reducing bias, synthesising evidence and improving clinical practice through evidence-based medicine. Implementation science has similarly generated valuable insights into how evidence can be integrated within healthcare organisations and professional practice. However, the persistence of preventable disease and the growing global burden of lifestyle-related chronic conditions suggest that further progress requires attention to a complementary question: how people themselves engage with evidence throughout the journey from knowledge generation to health creation.^9–14^

This perspective therefore views the Evidence-to-Health Gap as a systems challenge rather than a failure of any single stakeholder. Researchers, healthcare professionals, policymakers, educators, communities and individuals each contribute essential components of the health ecosystem. Nevertheless, the effectiveness of the system depends upon how well these components work together to support the person whose daily decisions ultimately determine whether evidence becomes action. The challenge is not simply to move evidence into practice, but to enable evidence to become part of people's lives.

The concept of partnership provides a useful lens through which this challenge can be understood. Many existing models implicitly position people near the end of the evidence pathway, after priorities have been established, research questions defined, evidence generated and recommendations formulated. People are then expected to adopt behaviours, adhere to treatments or comply with guidelines that have largely been developed without their continuous involvement. Although patient and public involvement in research has expanded considerably in recent years, meaningful partnership remains uneven across disciplines and stages of the research-to-practice continuum.^15–18^

Consequently, opportunities are frequently missed to improve the relevance, acceptability and usability of scientific evidence. Research priorities may differ from community priorities. Outcome measures valued by investigators may not fully capture what matters most to people living with chronic conditions. Evidence summaries may remain inaccessible to non-specialist audiences. Recommended interventions may be difficult to implement within the realities of work, family responsibilities or limited resources. These disconnects do not diminish the quality of the science itself; rather, they reduce its ability to generate measurable improvements in health and LifeQuality.

The challenge is particularly evident in Lifestyle Medicine. Strong evidence supports the effectiveness of healthy dietary patterns, regular physical activity, restorative sleep, avoidance of tobacco, moderation of alcohol, stress management and positive social connections in preventing and managing many chronic diseases.^19–22^ Yet sustained adoption of these behaviours remains difficult for many individuals despite widespread awareness of their benefits. This observation suggests that dissemination of information alone is insufficient. Behaviour is influenced by complex interactions among capability, opportunity, motivation, social context, environment and personal meaning. Successful translation therefore requires approaches that acknowledge this complexity rather than assuming that knowledge automatically produces action.^23–25^

A similar pattern is observed in public health, where interventions addressing vaccination, screening, obesity, cardiovascular disease and other major health challenges often achieve variable uptake despite strong supporting evidence. Social trust, cultural beliefs, health literacy, economic circumstances and community engagement all influence whether evidence-informed interventions are accepted and sustained. Effective implementation therefore depends not only on scientific validity but also on relationships, communication and partnership between institutions and the populations they serve.^26–28^

These observations lead to a broader interpretation of translation. Rather than viewing translation as the movement of evidence from researchers to clinicians and then to patients, it may be better understood as a continuous collaborative process in which scientific knowledge is refined, contextualised, implemented, evaluated and improved through ongoing interaction among all participants. Evidence remains essential, but its impact depends increasingly upon the quality of these relationships.

### Central Partnership Hypothesis

Against this background, we propose the following central hypothesis:

**The persistent Evidence-to-Health Gap may be partly explained by insufficient partnership with people throughout the evidence-to-action continuum.**

This hypothesis is intended as a conceptual proposition rather than an established empirical conclusion. It complements, rather than replaces, existing explanations derived from implementation science, behavioural science, health systems research and public health. We suggest that many recognised barriers to evidence translation share a common underlying feature: people are too often engaged primarily as recipients of recommendations rather than as partners in the processes through which health is created.

Within the proposed framework, partnership begins before research is undertaken. People help identify the problems that matter most, contribute to defining research priorities and participate, where appropriate, in the design and conduct of research. They contribute to interpreting findings, ensuring that evidence is synthesised into forms that are openly accessible, understandable and relatable to people's situations and needs. They participate in implementing evidence within the realities of everyday life and contribute to continuous learning by sharing experiences, outcomes and contextual insights that inform future improvements.

This expanded understanding of partnership reflects the proposition that health is ultimately created in people's daily lives. People are the primary agents of health creation. Scientific research, healthcare professionals and health systems exist to support, enable and strengthen people's capacity to create, preserve and restore health.

Importantly, partnership should not be confused with transferring responsibility from institutions to individuals. Health creation remains a shared endeavour. Researchers remain responsible for producing rigorous evidence. Healthcare professionals remain responsible for providing competent, ethical and compassionate care. Health systems remain responsible for ensuring equitable access, quality and safety. Governments retain responsibility for creating environments that support healthy choices. Communities contribute social support and collective action. Partnership therefore strengthens rather than weakens institutional responsibility by aligning all participants around the common objective of enabling healthier lives.

At the same time, partnership combines respect with accountability: empowering people while acknowledging their shared responsibility for the choices through which health and LifeQuality are progressively created. Rights and responsibilities are inseparable. Respect for autonomy is accompanied by recognition that many determinants of health lie within everyday decisions, while acknowledging that people's capacity to act is shaped by broader social, economic and environmental conditions. Partnership therefore seeks neither paternalism nor individualism, but shared responsibility grounded in mutual respect.

If this hypothesis is valid, its implications extend beyond healthcare delivery. It suggests that evidence generation, knowledge synthesis, professional education, health communication, digital health, community engagement and public policy should all be designed with partnership as a foundational principle. Such an orientation has the potential to strengthen not only implementation but also the relevance, accessibility, trustworthiness and sustainability of health improvement efforts across the entire evidence-to-action continuum.

The following section develops this proposition into a conceptual model that positions partnership as the integrating mechanism through which scientific evidence, professional expertise, supportive health systems and people's everyday actions converge to create better health and improved LifeQuality.

** 3. Conceptual Model: Partnership Across the Evidence-to-Action Continuum

The central hypothesis presented in the preceding section proposes that the persistent Evidence-to-Health Gap may be partly explained by insufficient partnership with people throughout the evidence-to-action continuum. This section develops that hypothesis into a conceptual model that integrates existing approaches within a single, person-centred framework.

The model is founded on two propositions that underpin this Perspective. First, **health is ultimately created in people's daily lives.** Second, **people are the primary agents of health creation.** These propositions do not diminish the indispensable contributions of scientific research, healthcare professionals or health systems. Rather, they clarify their shared purpose: **scientific research, healthcare professionals and health systems exist to support, enable and strengthen people's capacity to create, preserve and restore health.**

Viewed through this lens, the conventional pathway from scientific discovery to improved health is incomplete. Traditional models frequently portray translation as a largely linear process in which research generates evidence, evidence informs guidelines, healthcare professionals deliver interventions, and people receive care. While valuable, this sequence implicitly positions people near the end of the process, primarily as recipients of evidence or healthcare services.

The partnership model proposed here reconceptualises this pathway as a continuous, bidirectional and learning-oriented process in which people participate throughout the entire evidence-to-action continuum. Partnership begins with identifying the health challenges that matter most to individuals and communities, continues through evidence generation and interpretation, extends to the synthesis and communication of knowledge, and culminates in implementation, evaluation and continuous improvement. At every stage, people contribute unique experiential knowledge that complements scientific and professional expertise.

The resulting model is neither hierarchical nor transactional. Instead, it represents a collaborative ecosystem in which each stakeholder contributes distinct but complementary capabilities. Researchers contribute scientific rigour and discovery. Healthcare professionals contribute clinical expertise, ethical practice and compassionate care. Health systems create structures that facilitate equitable, high-quality services. Public health agencies establish supportive policies and environments. Communities provide social support and collective capacity. People integrate all of these resources within the realities of everyday life through the choices and actions by which health is ultimately created.

Importantly, the model recognises that evidence generation and evidence application are not separate activities but components of a continuous learning cycle. Experiences arising from implementation generate new questions, identify practical barriers, reveal contextual influences and expose evidence gaps that inform future research. Partnership therefore transforms people from passive recipients of scientific knowledge into active contributors to an iterative system of health creation and continuous learning.

This conceptualisation aligns with contemporary developments in learning health systems, implementation science and participatory research while extending each beyond its traditional boundaries. Rather than treating partnership as a discrete intervention or engagement strategy, the present model proposes partnership as the organising principle that connects these complementary disciplines into a coherent framework for translating evidence into health.

## The Partnership Across the Evidence-to-Action Continuum Model

The proposed model comprises seven interconnected stages.

**1. Identifying priorities**

Health creation begins by understanding the needs, aspirations and challenges experienced by individuals and communities. Partnership at this stage ensures that research priorities and health policies address questions that matter most to the people whose health they are intended to improve.

**2. Generating evidence**

Scientific investigation remains the cornerstone of reliable knowledge. Researchers, clinicians, communities and people collaborate, where appropriate, in designing studies, selecting meaningful outcomes and ensuring that research addresses real-world contexts without compromising scientific rigour.

**3. Synthesising knowledge**

Evidence should be synthesised into forms that are openly accessible, understandable and relatable to people's situations and needs. Systematic reviews, clinical guidelines, decision aids and digital knowledge platforms all contribute to this process, but effective synthesis also requires contextual interpretation that enables informed action.

**4. Shared interpretation and decision making**

Evidence gains practical meaning through dialogue among people, healthcare professionals and other stakeholders. Scientific evidence, professional expertise and individual values are integrated to determine actions that are both evidence-informed and personally relevant.

**5. Health creation in daily life**

The central stage of the model occurs beyond formal healthcare settings. Health is ultimately created in people's daily lives through everyday decisions concerning nutrition, physical activity, sleep, stress management, social relationships, adherence to treatment, environmental interactions and other determinants of health. These actions are influenced by personal capability, motivation, opportunity, culture, community and socioeconomic conditions.

**6. Evaluation and feedback**

Implementation generates experiences and outcomes that should be systematically evaluated. People contribute essential feedback regarding feasibility, acceptability, barriers, facilitators and outcomes that may not be captured by conventional clinical measures alone.

**7. Continuous improvement**

Knowledge derived from evaluation informs future research, clinical practice, policy development and community initiatives. The cycle then recommences with improved understanding, creating a continuously learning ecosystem that progressively narrows the Evidence-to-Health Gap.

This framework positions partnership not as an isolated activity but as the connective mechanism operating throughout every stage of health creation.

## Definitions

For clarity and consistency, the following definitions are used throughout this Perspective.

**Evidence-to-Health Gap**

The difference between what scientific evidence demonstrates can improve health and the health outcomes ultimately realised by individuals and populations through everyday life.

**Health Creation**

The continuous process through which health is created, preserved and restored through interactions among individuals, communities, healthcare professionals, health systems and the broader determinants of health, with its ultimate expression occurring in people's daily lives.

**Partnership**

A collaborative relationship in which people, researchers, healthcare professionals, health systems and communities contribute their respective knowledge, expertise and responsibilities throughout the evidence-to-action continuum to improve health and LifeQuality.

**People**

Individuals, families, carers and communities who actively participate in creating, preserving and restoring health.

**LifeQuality**

The overall quality of life experienced by individuals as they pursue meaningful, productive and fulfilling lives. The term encompasses, but is not limited to, health-related quality of life and recognises physical, mental, social and functional well-being as interconnected dimensions of human flourishing.

**Evidence-to-Action Continuum**

The continuous process through which health priorities are identified, evidence is generated, synthesised, interpreted, implemented, evaluated and refined to create better health and improved LifeQuality.

** 4. Historical Evolution and Existing Foundations

The partnership model proposed in this Perspective does not emerge in isolation. Rather than introducing an entirely new paradigm, it seeks to integrate and extend several influential movements that have progressively reshaped thinking about health, healthcare and the translation of scientific knowledge into practice. Over the past five decades, disciplines including evidence-based medicine, health promotion, public health, implementation science, shared decision making, lifestyle medicine, participatory research and learning health systems have each contributed important insights into how health can be improved. Collectively, these developments reveal an evolution from viewing people primarily as recipients of healthcare towards recognising their increasingly active role in creating health.

Although these disciplines have developed largely independently and with different objectives, they converge around a common recognition: lasting improvements in health require more than scientific discovery and effective healthcare delivery. They require meaningful engagement with the people whose lives ultimately determine whether evidence is translated into better health. The partnership framework proposed here builds upon these foundations by integrating them around a single organising principle—that health is ultimately created in people's daily lives and that people are the primary agents of health creation.

## Historical Evolution

During much of the twentieth century, healthcare was predominantly organised around the diagnosis and treatment of disease. Scientific progress produced extraordinary advances in microbiology, pharmacology, surgery and diagnostic technologies, contributing to substantial reductions in mortality and improvements in life expectancy. Healthcare systems evolved accordingly, focusing primarily on professional expertise, hospital-based care and the management of acute illness.

As patterns of disease shifted towards chronic, multifactorial conditions, it became increasingly apparent that medical care alone could not fully explain differences in health outcomes. The publication of Lalonde's *A New Perspective on the Health of Canadians* in 1974 marked an important conceptual turning point by proposing that health is influenced not only by healthcare but also by human biology, environment and lifestyle.^1^ This broader perspective was subsequently reinforced by the World Health Organization's Ottawa Charter for Health Promotion, which emphasised enabling people to increase control over and improve their health.^2^

Subsequent decades witnessed growing recognition of the social determinants of health, behavioural science, community participation and health promotion as essential complements to clinical care.^3–5^ Simultaneously, advances in evidence-based medicine strengthened the scientific basis of healthcare, while implementation science emerged to address the persistent challenge of translating evidence into routine practice.^6–10^ More recently, learning health systems, patient and public involvement in research, citizen science and participatory approaches have further expanded opportunities for collaboration between science and society.^11–15^

Despite these important advances, many developments have evolved within disciplinary boundaries, each addressing specific components of the broader evidence-to-action continuum. The partnership model proposed here seeks to provide an overarching framework that connects these complementary traditions while preserving their individual strengths.

## Evidence-Based Medicine

Evidence-based medicine (EBM) transformed healthcare by promoting the conscientious, explicit and judicious use of the best available evidence in clinical decision making.^6^ Through systematic reviews, critical appraisal and evidence-based guidelines, EBM substantially improved the reliability, transparency and consistency of healthcare decisions.

Importantly, contemporary formulations of evidence-based medicine recognise that high-quality care requires the integration of three complementary elements: the best available research evidence, clinical expertise and patient values and preferences.^16^ This evolution has already moved EBM beyond a purely evidence-centric model towards one that acknowledges the importance of partnership within clinical encounters.

The present Perspective fully embraces the principles of evidence-based medicine. However, it proposes that the endpoint of evidence-based practice should not be viewed as evidence-informed clinical decisions alone, but as improvements in health and LifeQuality realised through people's daily lives. Partnership therefore extends the principles of EBM beyond clinical encounters to encompass the entire evidence-to-action continuum.

## Lifestyle Medicine

Lifestyle Medicine has demonstrated that many of the leading causes of premature mortality and chronic disease are substantially influenced by modifiable behaviours including nutrition, physical activity, sleep, stress management, avoidance of tobacco, moderation of alcohol consumption and positive social relationships.^17–20^ The evidence supporting these interventions is now extensive and continues to expand.

Yet Lifestyle Medicine also illustrates the central challenge addressed in this Perspective. Although effective lifestyle interventions are increasingly well established, sustained behaviour change remains difficult for many individuals and populations. The existence of compelling evidence alone rarely guarantees long-term implementation.

This observation reinforces the proposition that health is ultimately created in people's daily lives. Lifestyle Medicine therefore provides one of the strongest practical examples of why partnership is essential. Scientific evidence identifies effective behaviours, healthcare professionals provide guidance and support, health systems create enabling services, but individuals ultimately integrate these recommendations into everyday living within the context of their own values, opportunities and constraints.

Rather than viewing adherence primarily as patient compliance, the partnership framework emphasises collaborative health creation in which people actively participate in understanding, adapting and sustaining evidence-informed behaviours that improve health and LifeQuality.

## Implementation Science

Implementation science emerged in response to the well-recognised delay between scientific discovery and routine clinical practice.^8–10^ The discipline has generated valuable frameworks for understanding barriers and facilitators to evidence uptake, identifying implementation strategies and evaluating their effectiveness across healthcare settings.

The present Perspective is closely aligned with implementation science but differs in the scope of the outcome under consideration. Implementation science has traditionally focused on integrating evidence within healthcare organisations, professional practice and service delivery. By contrast, the Evidence-to-Health Gap extends beyond implementation within healthcare systems to encompass whether evidence ultimately contributes to healthier lives.

This distinction does not diminish the importance of implementation science; rather, it broadens its endpoint. Successful implementation should ultimately be judged not only by adoption within healthcare organisations but also by the degree to which evidence supports people in creating, preserving and restoring health in everyday life.

## Shared Decision Making

Shared decision making represents one of the clearest expressions of partnership within contemporary clinical practice. By integrating scientific evidence with professional expertise and patient values, it promotes informed decisions that respect autonomy while improving communication, trust and satisfaction.^21,22^

The partnership model proposed here builds directly upon this foundation while extending its scope beyond individual clinical decisions. People participate not only in choosing among treatment options but also in identifying research priorities, generating evidence, interpreting findings, translating knowledge, implementing evidence and continuously improving health-creating actions.

Shared decision making therefore represents an essential component of partnership but not its full expression. Partnership begins before clinical encounters and continues long after they conclude.

## Learning Health Systems

Learning health systems seek to create continuous cycles in which clinical care, data generation, research and quality improvement mutually reinforce one another.^11^ Digital technologies, electronic health records and real-world evidence increasingly enable healthcare systems to learn from routine practice and improve continuously.

The partnership framework complements this vision by recognising people not merely as sources of data but as active participants in learning. Their lived experiences, preferences, contextual knowledge and feedback contribute valuable forms of evidence that enrich scientific understanding and improve implementation.

Consequently, learning extends beyond healthcare institutions to include communities and everyday life, creating a broader ecosystem of continuous improvement centred on health creation rather than healthcare delivery alone.

## Public Health and Health Promotion

Public health has long recognised that health is shaped by environmental, social, economic and behavioural determinants extending well beyond healthcare services.^2–5^ Health promotion similarly emphasises enabling people and communities to increase control over the determinants of health through supportive environments, healthy public policies and community action.

The partnership model proposed in this Perspective is strongly aligned with these principles. However, it seeks to strengthen the explicit connection between health promotion and evidence translation. Scientific evidence achieves its greatest value when it becomes understandable, relatable and actionable for the people whose lives it is intended to improve.

This emphasis also recognises that enabling healthier choices requires supportive environments and equitable opportunities. Partnership therefore does not imply that individuals bear sole responsibility for health. Rather, it acknowledges shared responsibility among governments, communities, healthcare systems, professionals and people themselves.

## Rights-Based Approaches

Contemporary healthcare increasingly recognises health as a fundamental human right and emphasises respect for dignity, autonomy, participation and equity.^23–25^ Rights-based approaches have contributed substantially to person-centred care, patient engagement and inclusive health policy.

The present Perspective fully supports these principles while proposing that rights and responsibilities are inseparable. Respect for individual autonomy is accompanied by recognition that people share responsibility for the choices through which health and LifeQuality are progressively created. At the same time, institutions retain corresponding responsibilities to provide trustworthy evidence, equitable healthcare, supportive environments and opportunities that enable healthier lives.

Partnership therefore combines respect with accountability: empowering people while acknowledging their shared responsibility for the choices through which health and LifeQuality are progressively created. This reciprocal understanding avoids both paternalism and excessive individualism, recognising health creation as a shared societal endeavour.

## Integrating Existing Foundations

Taken individually, each of these disciplines addresses an important component of the evidence-to-action continuum. Evidence-based medicine strengthens scientific validity. Lifestyle Medicine demonstrates the importance of everyday behaviours. Implementation science improves translation into practice. Shared decision making enhances clinical partnership. Learning health systems enable continuous improvement. Public health and health promotion address the broader determinants of health. Rights-based approaches reinforce dignity, participation and equity.

The partnership model proposed in this Perspective does not seek to replace these established fields. Rather, it provides an integrating conceptual framework that connects them around a shared objective: enabling people to create, preserve and restore health throughout their daily lives.

Viewed collectively, these disciplines suggest an ongoing evolution in healthcare thinking—from treating disease, to delivering evidence-based care, to implementing evidence effectively, and ultimately towards partnering with people in creating health. The proposed framework positions partnership as the unifying principle that links scientific discovery, professional expertise, supportive systems and people's everyday actions into a continuously learning ecosystem capable of narrowing the Evidence-to-Health Gap and improving LifeQuality.

** 5. Partnership in Practice, Research Agenda, Discussion and Conclusion

If the Evidence-to-Health Gap is partly explained by insufficient partnership with people throughout the evidence-to-action continuum, then partnership should become a practical organising principle rather than simply an ethical aspiration. Its implications extend across research, healthcare delivery, public health, education, digital health and health policy.

Within research, partnership begins by involving people in identifying priorities, refining research questions and selecting outcomes that reflect what matters most in everyday life. Patient and public involvement, citizen science and community-based participatory research have demonstrated that meaningful engagement can improve the relevance, acceptability and societal value of research without compromising scientific rigour.^1–4^ The present Perspective extends these approaches by proposing that partnership should be considered throughout the complete evidence-to-action continuum rather than at isolated stages of the research process.

For healthcare professionals, partnership involves moving beyond the traditional model of information delivery towards collaborative health creation. Clinical expertise remains indispensable, but its impact is strengthened when recommendations are developed through dialogue, adapted to personal circumstances and supported by shared decision making, health coaching and longitudinal relationships that recognise the realities of daily life.^5–7^ In this model, clinicians become not only providers of care but also facilitators of people's capacity to create, preserve and restore health.

Health systems similarly play a critical enabling role. Their responsibility extends beyond providing high-quality clinical services to creating environments that support prevention, continuity of care, interdisciplinary collaboration and equitable access to trustworthy information. Digital technologies, electronic health records, telehealth and artificial intelligence offer unprecedented opportunities to personalise evidence, facilitate communication and support continuous learning. However, technology should strengthen rather than replace human partnership. Digital innovation achieves its greatest value when it empowers people to understand, apply and contribute to evidence-informed health creation.

Public health agencies and governments also occupy essential positions within the partnership framework. Many determinants of health—including education, employment, housing, transportation, food systems and environmental conditions—lie beyond the direct influence of healthcare services. Policies that create healthier environments enable individuals to make healthier choices and reduce structural barriers that contribute to health inequities.^8–10^ Partnership therefore requires coordinated action across sectors, recognising that health creation is a shared societal endeavour.

Communities provide another indispensable dimension of partnership. Families, schools, workplaces, voluntary organisations and local networks influence health behaviours through social support, shared norms and collective action. Community engagement enhances trust, facilitates dissemination of evidence and strengthens resilience, particularly among populations facing social or economic disadvantage. These social assets should be recognised as integral components of the evidence-to-action continuum rather than external influences upon it.

Ultimately, however, the proposed framework returns to its central proposition: **health is ultimately created in people's daily lives.** Individuals integrate scientific evidence, professional guidance, social support and personal experience into countless decisions concerning nutrition, physical activity, sleep, stress management, medication adherence, social relationships and other determinants of health. These daily actions represent the point at which evidence becomes health. Scientific discovery, healthcare delivery and public policy achieve their intended purpose only insofar as they strengthen people's capacity to create, preserve and restore health.

We propose a Learning and Evidence for Action and Practice (LEAP) framework to actualize this concept.

## Research Agenda

The partnership hypothesis advanced in this Perspective is intentionally conceptual and therefore requires empirical evaluation. We propose several priorities for future research.

First, the concept of the **Evidence-to-Health Gap** should be operationalised and measured. Existing implementation outcomes evaluate adoption, fidelity and sustainability within healthcare settings, but comparatively few metrics assess whether evidence ultimately translates into improved health and LifeQuality in everyday living.^11,12^ Developing valid measures of this broader outcome would enable systematic evaluation of partnership-based approaches.

Second, future studies should examine whether partnership throughout the evidence-to-action continuum improves outcomes beyond conventional implementation strategies. Comparative evaluations could investigate whether involving people in priority setting, evidence generation, knowledge translation and continuous learning improves the relevance, acceptability, uptake and sustainability of evidence-informed interventions.

Third, methods are needed to synthesise evidence into forms that are openly accessible, understandable and relatable to people's situations and needs without compromising scientific integrity. Advances in digital communication, artificial intelligence and personalised decision support provide new opportunities for translating increasingly complex evidence into practical knowledge that supports informed action.

Fourth, research should explore methods for integrating experiential knowledge with scientific evidence. People's lived experiences provide contextual insights that complement quantitative research and may identify barriers, facilitators and outcomes not readily captured by traditional study designs. Appropriate methodological frameworks are needed to ensure that experiential knowledge enriches rather than replaces scientific evidence.

Fifth, longitudinal studies should evaluate whether partnership contributes to sustained improvements in health behaviours, clinical outcomes, health equity and LifeQuality. Such evaluations should include diverse populations and health systems to determine the generalisability and scalability of the proposed framework.

Finally, conceptual work should further refine the relationship between partnership, implementation science, learning health systems, public health and Lifestyle Medicine. Rather than creating another isolated discipline, future scholarship should seek greater theoretical integration across these complementary fields.

## Discussion

This Perspective proposes that the persistent **Evidence-to-Health Gap** may be partly explained by insufficient partnership with people throughout the evidence-to-action continuum. Although implementation science has substantially advanced understanding of how evidence can be incorporated into healthcare practice, and evidence-based medicine has strengthened the scientific basis of clinical decision making, translating evidence into healthier lives remains an enduring challenge. We suggest that one reason for this persistence is that people have too often been viewed primarily as recipients of healthcare rather than as partners in health creation.

The framework presented here builds upon rather than challenges established scientific traditions. Evidence-based medicine, implementation science, shared decision making, Lifestyle Medicine, public health, health promotion, participatory research and learning health systems each contribute indispensable insights into different components of the evidence-to-action continuum. The novelty of the present Perspective lies not in replacing these disciplines but in integrating them around a common organising principle: **people are the primary agents of health creation**.

The proposed framework also broadens the endpoint of evidence translation. Conventional models frequently focus on whether evidence is generated, disseminated, adopted or implemented. While these processes remain essential, they represent intermediate rather than ultimate outcomes. The objective of health research is not simply the production of evidence, nor the implementation of guidelines, but measurable improvements in health and **LifeQuality**. This distinction encourages researchers, healthcare professionals and policymakers to evaluate success according to the extent to which scientific knowledge strengthens people's capacity to create, preserve and restore health.

Importantly, partnership should not be interpreted as transferring responsibility for health from institutions to individuals. Such an interpretation would overlook the profound influence of social, economic and environmental determinants of health. Instead, partnership recognises reciprocal responsibilities. Researchers remain responsible for producing trustworthy evidence. Healthcare professionals remain responsible for delivering competent and compassionate care. Health systems remain responsible for providing equitable access to effective services. Governments remain responsible for creating supportive environments and reducing avoidable inequities. Communities contribute social capital, collective action and mutual support. Within this shared enterprise, people contribute their lived experience, values, aspirations and everyday actions through which health is ultimately created.

The framework also recognises that partnership combines respect with accountability: empowering people while acknowledging their shared responsibility for the choices through which health and LifeQuality are progressively created. Rights and responsibilities are inseparable. Respect for autonomy is strengthened—not diminished—when individuals are supported to make informed decisions and participate meaningfully in creating their own health.

Several limitations should be acknowledged. First, the central partnership hypothesis remains a conceptual proposition that requires empirical validation. Second, the framework intentionally synthesises multiple disciplines and therefore cannot address every theoretical nuance within each field. Third, implementation of partnership will necessarily differ across healthcare systems, cultures and socioeconomic contexts. Finally, partnership alone cannot eliminate structural inequities or compensate for inadequate resources. It should therefore be viewed as one essential component of a broader systems approach to improving health.

These limitations notwithstanding, the framework offers a coherent conceptual foundation for future research and practice. By connecting existing disciplines through a common understanding of health creation, it provides a basis for developing more integrated approaches to evidence translation that place people at the centre of the health ecosystem.

## Conclusion

Scientific knowledge has never been more abundant, yet translating evidence into sustained improvements in individual and population health remains one of the defining challenges of contemporary healthcare. The persistent Evidence-to-Health Gap suggests that excellence in research and healthcare delivery, while essential, may not by themselves be sufficient to realise the full potential of scientific discovery.

This Perspective proposes that the persistent **Evidence-to-Health Gap** may be partly explained by insufficient partnership with people throughout the evidence-to-action continuum. The proposed framework is founded upon two complementary propositions: **health is ultimately created in people's daily lives**, and **people are the primary agents of health creation**. From this perspective, scientific research, healthcare professionals and health systems exist to support, enable and strengthen people's capacity to create, preserve and restore health.

Partnership extends beyond consultation, participation or shared decision making alone. People participate not only in applying evidence but also in defining the problems, asking the questions, generating the evidence, translating the knowledge and continuously improving the actions through which healthier lives are created. Evidence should be synthesised into forms that are openly accessible, understandable and relatable to people's situations and needs. Partnership combines respect with accountability: empowering people while acknowledging their shared responsibility for the choices through which health and LifeQuality are progressively created. Rights and responsibilities are inseparable.

Rather than replacing established disciplines, the proposed framework integrates evidence-based medicine, Lifestyle Medicine, implementation science, public health, health promotion, shared decision making, participatory research and learning health systems within a common conceptual model centred on partnership. In doing so, it reframes the objective of evidence translation from implementing evidence within healthcare systems to enabling people to create healthier, more fulfilling lives.

If this perspective stimulates further empirical investigation, conceptual refinement and practical innovation, it may contribute to a broader evolution in healthcare thinking—from viewing people primarily as recipients of healthcare towards recognising them as partners in health creation. Narrowing the Evidence-to-Health Gap will require not only better science and better healthcare, but stronger partnerships through which scientific knowledge becomes healthier lives and improved LifeQuality.
